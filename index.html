1.1 REFLECTIONS ON THE COURSE OF LANGUAGE ACQUISITION

Human language acquisition is an astonishing process that unfolds over about 3 years.

For children, acquiring a language is an effortless achievement that occurs

  • without explicit teaching,
  • on the basis of positive evidence (i.e., what they hear),1
  • under varying circumstances, and in a limited amount of time,
  • in identical ways across different languages.

1.1.1 Acquiring Language without Explicit Teaching

  • Acquiring a first or native language differs significantly from learning a second language in adulthood.

  • Unlike formal instruction for second languages, children acquiring their native language do not receive systematic teaching from parents.

    • Parents typically do not teach children explicit language rules or restrictions on sentence structures.

    • Children's first language develops spontaneously through exposure to linguistic input.

    • Children are rarely corrected, and even when corrected, they often resist the correction.
    • Example: English-speaking children persistently saying "goed" instead of "went" despite occasional correction.

Case Example - Double Negation:

  • McNeill (1966) provides a dialogue illustrating the challenge of correction:
    • Child uses double negation ("nobody don't").
    • Despite repeated corrections, the child continues with the incorrect form.
    • Corrective feedback does not lead to immediate and complete adoption of the corrected form.

Conclusion:

  • Acquiring a first language is primarily a spontaneous process driven by exposure to linguistic input.

  • Parents generally do not provide systematic instruction, and correction may not lead to immediate and comprehensive changes in language use by children.

    This summary highlights the contrast between language acquisition in children and learning a second language in adulthood, emphasizing the spontaneous nature of first language development and the limited impact of corrective feedback on linguistic nuances.

1.1.2 Acquiring Language on the Basis of Positive Evidence

  • Language acquisition relies on positive evidence, which involves exposure to linguistic input rather than explicit correction.
  • Corrections from parents, termed negative evidence, are infrequent and may not significantly impact children's linguistic behavior.

Limited Impact of Negative Evidence:

Rare Corrections

  • Corrections from parents about language rules (negative evidence) are uncommon.

  • Negative evidence aims to inform children about linguistic limitations but is not consistently provided.

Debated Effectiveness:

  • Research explores whether negative evidence is conveyed through parents' disapproval, failure to understand, or expansion of children's speech.
  • General consensus: Negative evidence is not consistently available, often noisy, and deemed insufficient for comprehensive language learning.

Unreliable Source:

  • Negative evidence is not a reliable informational source for language acquisition.
  • Various studies (Brown and Hanlon 1970; Bowerman 1988; Morgan and Travis 1989; Marcus 1993) support the limited impact of negative evidence.

Emphasis on Positive Evidence:

  • Children's optimal language acquisition occurs through positive evidence, i.e., exposure to the linguistic environment around them.
  • Positive evidence is abundant and provides a consistent resource for language learning.

Conclusion:

  • Language acquisition, facilitated by positive evidence, relies on exposure to linguistic input rather than explicit correction.
  • Negative evidence, though explored in research, is found to be limited, noisy, and unreliable, emphasizing the crucial role of positive evidence in children's language development.

This summary underscores the importance of positive evidence in language acquisition and highlights the limitations of negative evidence in shaping children's linguistic behavior.

1.1.3 Acquiring Language under Varying Circumstances and in a Limited Amount of Time

  • Children acquire language under diverse circumstances, with variations in linguistic input across individuals.
  • Despite these differences, children achieve the same linguistic competence within a relatively short timeframe.

Diverse Circumstances

  1. Variability in Linguistic Input:
    • Children experience diverse linguistic environments, as seen in the acquisition of American Sign Language and creoles (refer to section 1.5.3).

Timely Mastery

  1. Uniform Competence:

    • Regardless of varied circumstances, all children attain linguistic competence.
    • This competence is achieved within a limited timeframe, showcasing the efficiency of language acquisition.

  2. Milestone by 5 Years:

    • By around 5 years of age, children master most language constructions.
    • While vocabulary continues to expand, the foundational language structures are acquired within this time frame.

Conclusion

  • Language acquisition in children occurs under diverse circumstances, adapting to different linguistic inputs.
  • Despite these variations, children universally achieve linguistic competence within a limited time frame, typically mastering language constructions by the age of 5.

1.1.4 Acquiring Language in Identical Ways across Different Languages

  • Children achieve linguistic milestones in a parallel fashion, irrespective of the specific language they are exposed to.
  • Common developmental stages are observed across various languages, demonstrating similarities in the timing and content of language acquisition.

Linguistic Milestones

  1. Babbling Phase (6–8 months):

    • All children, regardless of language, begin babbling, producing repetitive syllables like "bababa" (refer to chapter 2).

  2. First Words (10–12 months):

    • Children typically start speaking their first words around 10–12 months of age.

  3. Word Combination (20–24 months):

    • Between 20 and 24 months, children begin to put words together, marking a crucial stage in language development.

  4. Commonalities Across Languages (2–3 years):

    • Children, between 2 and 3 years old, exhibit common linguistic features across a variety of languages.
      • Use of infinitive verbs in main clauses (refer to chapter 4).
      • Omission of sentential subjects (refer to chapter 5).

  5. Overregularization (Across Languages):

    • Young children, irrespective of language, tend to overregularize irregular verbs, including past tense forms.

  6. Similarities Across Modalities (Spoken and Signed Languages):

    • Similar linguistic milestones observed not only across spoken languages but also between spoken and signed languages.
      • Example: Deaf babies, like hearing babies, start babbling manually (Petitto 1995).

Striking Similarities

  • Despite variations in linguistic input and acquisition conditions, the timing and milestones of language acquisition are remarkably similar across different languages and modalities.
  • Early language content is virtually identical, showcasing the universality of certain linguistic developmental stages.

1.2 THE LOGICAL PROBLEM OF LANGUAGE ACQUISITION

  • Researchers, as characterized by Baker and McCarthy (1981), present the problem of language acquisition based on several key observations.

Key Characteristics

  1. Rich Linguistic Knowledge:

    • Children acquire extensive linguistic knowledge encompassing a potentially infinite number of sentences, despite exposure to a finite number of sentences.

  2. Positive Evidence Basis:

    • Children rely on positive evidence, i.e., sentences acceptable in their exposed language, for language acquisition.

  3. Implicit Correction:

    • Children are not explicitly informed about ill-formed sentences or the limitations on sentence interpretations. However, they eventually attain this knowledge, demonstrating the ability to judge sentence acceptability.

  4. Absence of Expected Errors:

    • Despite making errors, children avoid certain errors that might be expected if they generalized from the linguistic input.
      • Example: Children do not generalize from sentences like "Who do you wanna invite?" to impossible English sentences like "*Who do you wanna come?" (see section 1.4).

Argument from the Poverty of the Stimulus

  • These points contribute to the argument from the poverty of the stimulus, suggesting that certain abstract linguistic properties known by all speakers cannot be induced from the available positive evidence for children.

Exploring Linguistic Knowledge Origins

  • Given the premises and the argument from the poverty of the stimulus, the central question arises: Where does linguistic knowledge come from?
  • The exploration of this question follows a brief excursion into background assumptions.

1.3 The Notion of Grammar

To know a language means to possess a system of knowledge called grammar. Grammar is a finite system represented in the mind/brain. As Chomsky demonstrated in the 1950s, it is a mental generative procedure using finite means to generate an indefinite number of sentences.

The term grammar, in this context, refers to a psychological entity rather than an inventory of sounds, morphemes, inflectional paradigms, and syntactic constructions. Terms like passive, relative clause, and interrogative are used for convenience and do not have independent status.

Basic Concepts

  • Our linguistic knowledge enables us to produce and understand sentences we've never heard before.
  • It allows us to determine the acceptability of sentences in our language.

Sentence Acceptability Example:

  • Unacceptable Sentence: "Dog a old a bone ate." (Example: (2))

    This sentence is comprehensible but violates the rules of English grammar, making it unacceptable.

  • Acceptable Sentence with Ambiguity: "Mary washed her." (Example: (3))

    The pronoun "her" in (3) must refer to an individual distinct from Mary. The sentence is sound only on the interpretation that Mary washed another female individual, not herself.

  • Ambiguous Sentence: "Mary washes her socks." (Example: (4))

    The pronoun "her" can be interpreted in two ways: referring to the same individual as Mary or another salient individual in the context.

Ambiguity Example:

  • Ambiguous Sentence: "John kept the car in the garage." (Example: (5))

    • Interpretation (6a): The car that John kept was the one in the garage.
    • Interpretation (6b): The garage was where John kept the car.

Our ability to interpret sentences is based on the different structural representations associated with them by our mental grammar. Constraints determine what is possible and not possible in language.

1.4 Constraints

Constraints in linguistics are principles that prohibit certain arrangements of words, operations, and associations of sounds and meanings. They encode properties that are universally applicable across languages and are considered inviolable. Unlike in Optimality Theory, where constraints can be violable and ranked, the framework adopted here treats constraints as equally important with no ranking among them.

Constraints on Form

  • Definition: Constraints on form encode information about sentence ill-formedness.

Example 1: Question Formation

  • Ill-formed Sentence: *How does John regret that John behaved? (Example: (8b))

  • Well-formed Sentence: How does John think that Paul behaved? (Example: (9b))

    A minimal variant of the ill-formed sentence is well formed.

Example 2: Wanna-Contraction

  • Contractible: Who do you wanna invite? (Example: (10a))

  • Non-contractible: Who do you wanna come? (Example: (12a))

    Wanna-contraction is prohibited when the questioned element is the subject of the infinitival clause.

Constraints on Meaning

  • Definition: Constraints on meaning govern the interpretations speakers assign to acceptable sentences.

Example: Anaphoric Interpretation

  • Ambiguous Sentence: *He danced, while John was singing. (Example: (13a))

  • Unambiguous Sentence: While he was singing, John was dancing. (Example: (13b))

    The constraint on the interpretation of the pronoun "he" prohibits an anaphoric interpretation in certain structural configurations, making (13a) ruled out when the pronoun refers to the same individual as John.

Universal Constraints

  • Linguistic knowledge about form and meaning is expressed in terms of constraints that are universal and non-violable.
  • Constraints guide linguistic behavior, allowing speakers to judge the licitness of linguistic constructs and interpret pronouns based on context.

1.5 Where Does Knowledge of Language Come From?

How do we acquire the ability to determine if a sentence is ill-formed, comprehend its meaning, or recognize ambiguity? Several hypotheses have been proposed, including imitation (section 1.5.1), reinforcement (section 1.5.2), association procedures (section 1.5.3), and Universal Grammar (section 1.5.4).

1.5.1 Language Learning through Imitation

One hypothesis suggests that children learn language by imitating adults. However, evidence contradicts this idea:

  • Parental Influence: Studies show that children are not heavily influenced by caregivers' speech styles. Parents' utterances, predominantly questions and commands, do not align with the simple declaratives that children primarily produce.

  • Novel Utterances: Children produce novel utterances, demonstrating their ability to generate sentences they have never heard before. For instance, English learners over-regularize irregular past tense verbs, using forms like "goed" and "singed" that they haven't encountered in adult speech.

  • Unheard Sentence Structures: Children construct sentences that they cannot have heard from adults, indicating a drive to discover linguistic rules. Examples include negative questions (Example: (15)) and long-distance extraction questions (Example: (16)).

    (15) a. What does he doesn't eat?

    b. Why could he couldn't wash his hands?

    (16) What do you think what the puppet has eaten?

These instances suggest that imitation does not play a crucial role in language acquisition. Children actively go beyond their linguistic input to explore and internalize the rules of their language.

1.5.2 Language Learning through Reinforcement

Behaviorist psychologists propose that language is learned through the reinforcement mechanism, involving the contingent association between stimulus and response. This general-purpose mechanism is often used to explain various learning processes in animals and humans, as outlined by Skinner (1957). According to this view, children learn language by being positively reinforced for correct verbal expressions and negatively reinforced for errors.

Limitations of the Reinforcement Hypothesis

However, this hypothesis faces several challenges:

Inability to Explain Language Complexity:

  • Children acquire competence over an indefinite number of sentences, understanding and producing sentences they have never encountered. The reinforcement hypothesis struggles to account for this expansive linguistic proficiency.

Attention to Content Over Form:

  • Parents generally pay attention to the content of what children say rather than its grammatical correctness. Responses to children's questions (Example: (17) and (18)) demonstrate that reinforcement is vague. While responding, the mother reinforces the child's communication by acknowledging understanding, but this does not necessarily indicate grammatical correctness.

    (17) Adam: Where penny go? (Adam, 2;5)

    Mother: I don't know.

    (18) Adam: Where penny go? (Adam, 2;5)

    Mother: Didn't you drop your pennies on the floor?

Ineffectiveness of Corrections:

  • Even when parents correct children's ungrammatical sentences, corrections often go unnoticed, as discussed in section 1.1.1. Positive and negative reinforcement alone cannot explain the development of human linguistic competence.

In summary, the reinforcement hypothesis falls short in explaining the complexity of language acquisition and the nuanced nature of linguistic competence. Other factors, such as the ability to understand and produce novel sentences, suggest that reinforcement mechanisms alone are insufficient.

1.5.3 Language Learning through Association

Connectionism

An alternative hypothesis for language acquisition is connectionism, also known as neural networks or parallel distributed processing. However, this approach has faced criticism, and debates persist about its ability to comprehensively explain the intricate aspects of language acquisition.

Connectionist Models

Connectionist models reject the representation of symbols and rules in brain circuits, often opposing models that manipulate symbols. They are inspired by a coarse metaphor of the brain, consisting of interconnected neuron-like processing units modified by learning associations between input (stimulus) and output (response) patterns.

Types of Models

  1. Symbol-Free Models: Reject symbolic representation in favor of neural connections.
  2. Neural Substrate Models: Aim to explain how symbolic manipulations can be implemented in a neural substrate.

Application to English Past Tense Acquisition

Connectionist models attempt to simulate how children acquire the past tense in English, with distinctions between regular and irregular verbs.

Regular Verbs

  • Form past tense by adding "-ed" to the stem.
  • Learning associations between phonetic properties of verb stems and past forms.

Irregular Verbs

  • Grouped in family resemblance patterns with idiosyncratic past forms.
  • Generalizing associations to form past tenses for similar-sounding words.

Criticisms and Discrepancies

  • Critiques involve discrepancies between model simulations and children's actual learning.
  • Overregularization errors, especially for vowel-change verbs, differ between models and children.

Morphological and Syntactic Aspects

Connectionist models attempt to simulate aspects of morphological acquisition and, to a limited extent, syntax.

Morphological Acquisition

  • Simulations include overregularization errors in past tense formation.

Syntax Simulation

  • Some attempts to simulate aspects of syntax, such as sequencing of words.
  • Challenges remain in capturing linguistic constraints.

Word-and-Rule Theory

Pinker's word-and-rule theory combines rule-based and associative components for inflection, distinguishing between regular and irregular words.

  • Regular Words: Rule-based in¯ection (e.g., adding "-ed" to form past tense).
  • Irregular Words: Associative mechanisms based on sound similarity.

Limitations and Criticisms

Despite its attempts, connectionism struggles to explain various linguistic phenomena, prompting discussions about the need for additional mechanisms in language acquisition theories.

Incomplete Explanation

  • Challenges in explaining the entirety of language acquisition.
  • Additional mechanisms may be required.

Human Learning from Degenerate Input

  • Examples: Creole languages and sign languages provide evidence of humans acquiring language from degenerate input (Bickerton 1988; Goldin-Meadow and Mylander 1984; Kegl 1994).

  • Creole Development: In the 19th century, people in plantations and slave colonies developed rudimentary languages, known as lingua franca or pidgin. When native speakers (children) emerge, the pidgin evolves into a creole with function morphemes and a more complex structure.

  • Sign Language Acquisition: Deaf children born to late learners of American Sign Language (ASL) receive rudimentary input, omitting complex structures and function morphemes. Despite this, children achieve a more refined competence, expanding the language.

Connectionist Models' Limitations

  • Inability to Simulate Human Skill: Connectionist models face challenges in simulating the human ability to expand and refine language from degenerate input.

  • Learning Exclusively from Input: Connectionist systems learn solely based on input. If the input is degenerate, the model learns a degenerate language.

  • Need for Innate Structures: To simulate the expansion and refinement observed in human learning, connectionist models would require a program that introduces innate components. This implies the recognition that language acquisition involves innate (possibly language-specific) structures.

Implications for Language Acquisition Theories

  • Role of Innate Structures: Human capacity to refine language from degenerate input suggests the presence of innate structures guiding the learning process.

  • Challenge to Connectionist Paradigm: The inability of connectionist models to replicate this human skill raises questions about the sufficiency of association-based learning mechanisms in explaining the full scope of language acquisition.

In summary, connectionist models, relying on association, encounter challenges in explaining the entirety of language acquisition. This suggests the need for theories that incorporate innate structures, rule-based mechanisms, and constraints.

1.5.4 The Innateness Hypothesis

Recall the premises of the argument from the poverty of the stimulus: that all speakers of a language know a fairly abstract property and that this property cannot be induced from the evidence available to children (positive evidence). The conclusion drawn from these premises, as argued by Chomsky (1959) and supported by the poverty of the stimulus, is that linguistic knowledge is inborn. This hypothesis challenges behaviorist views that hypothesize learning through imitation, reinforcement, and association.

Innate Knowledge and Constraints

Rich Genetic Makeup:

  • Researchers in the Chomskian tradition assert that inborn human knowledge is richly structured and consists of constraints or their equivalent effects. The universality of these constraints across languages suggests an innate origin, as it would be unlikely for all languages to conform to these constraints without a genetic basis.

  • Quote: "Languages 'are all basically set up in the way that human biology expects them to be'" (Gleitman and Lieberman 1995, xxi).

Expectations of Children:

  • Children are born with certain expectations about language structure, anticipating that any language they hear will align with the properties dictated by their genetic equipment.

Role of Universal Grammar (UG):

  • Universal Grammar (UG) is the term used to describe the set of constraints inherent in all humans at birth, shaping the course of language acquisition. UG defines the boundaries of possible linguistic variation and characterizes the notion of possible human language.

Possible Variation:

  • While languages exhibit variation, it is not unlimited. UG establishes the range of possible variation, providing a framework for understanding the initial linguistic state of human beings.

Interaction between Innate Factors and Environment

Acquisition Process:

  • The nativist view posits that language acquisition results from the interaction between inborn factors and the environment. Language is not strictly learned but is expected to emerge under normal conditions when a child is exposed to spoken or signed language.

Lexicon and Regularities:

  • While children need to learn the lexicon of their language, they also engage in figuring out the regularities of their language and how innate constructs manifest in their linguistic environment.

1.5.5 The Principles-and-Parameters Model

The genetic endowment of individuals equips them to learn any human language. Whether raised in an English-speaking, Italian-speaking, or Tibetan-speaking environment, individuals acquire the language prevalent in their surroundings. While all languages share a basic underlying structure, they exhibit variations, such as word order and the expression of sentential subjects.

Principles-and-Parameters Model

Two Types of Constraints:

  • The principles-and-parameters model, proposed by Chomsky (1981), posits that Universal Grammar (UG) comprises two types of constraints: principles and parameters. This model provides an explanation for both the universal properties and the variations observed across languages.

Principles:

  • Principles encode invariant properties that are universal across languages. For example, the constraint discussed in section 1.4 regulating the interpretation of pronouns is a principle, applicable in any human language.

Parameters:

  • Parameters encode properties that vary from one language to another, acting as switches that can be turned on or off. An example is the pro-drop or null subject parameter, which governs the phonological expression of the sentential subject.

Example Parameter:

  • A first approximation of a parameter, such as the pro-drop parameter, can be formulated as follows: "Can the sentential subject be phonologically null?" The answer to this question varies depending on the specific language.

Language Acquisition in Principles-and-Parameters Model

Innate Endowment:

  • Children are innately endowed with both principles and parameters, as these are given by UG. Their task in language acquisition is to set the parameters to the values specified by the language of their environment.

Selective Theory:

  • The theory of language acquisition endorsed here is selective rather than instructive. In a selective theory, a stimulus operates on what is already inborn, and the process involves selecting and amplifying components of pre-existing circuitry.

Biological Analogies:

  • Analogies to selective theories exist in other biological systems, such as the selective theory of antibody formation proposed by Niels Kaj Jerne.

UG represents the human genetic endowment responsible for language acquisition. It encompasses principles and parameters defining both invariant and variant properties of languages. Parameters delineate the range of linguistic variation possible, and together, principles and parameters define the concept of "possible human language." Language acquisition is a selective process where children set parameter values based on their linguistic environment.

1.6 THE CRITICAL PERIOD

Innate behaviors often exhibit critical periods, during which the ability to acquire competence reaches its peak, followed by a decline in the ability to acquire that competence. This phenomenon is observed in various aspects of behavior in animals, such as visual abilities, imprinting in ducks, attachment in young species, and song learning in birds.

Critical Periods in Language Acquisition

  • Lenneberg's Hypothesis:

    • Lenneberg (1967) proposed that language can fully develop only if it is acquired before puberty. Subsequent evidence has supported the idea that a native competence in language is attained only if language acquisition occurs before puberty.

  • Case of Genie:

    • Cases of children deprived of linguistic and social interaction during childhood, like Genie, provide evidence for the critical period hypothesis. Genie, discovered at the age of 13 after years of isolation, exhibited limited language abilities, especially in syntax, even after linguistic rehabilitation.

  • Congenitally Deaf Individuals:

    • Studies on congenitally deaf individuals exposed to American Sign Language (ASL) at different ages show a linear decline in performance with the age of first exposure. Early exposure, from birth, results in better performance, while later exposure, after age 12, leads to reduced competence.

  • Foreign Accent and Second Language Acquisition:

    • Research indicates that a foreign accent is detectable in individuals exposed to a foreign language at age 3, and accents become more pronounced with increasing age of first exposure. Similarly, exposure to American English as a second language before age 7 is crucial for achieving native competence in morphology and syntax.

Summary

  • The current evidence supports the existence of critical period effects for acquiring the phonology, morphology, and syntax of one's native language.
  • Although individuals are innately endowed with a richly structured system of linguistic knowledge, this system can fully develop only when exposed to appropriate stimuli early in life.
  • The natural achievement of acquiring a language, whether native or foreign, is more effortless for children and becomes increasingly challenging with age.

KEYWORDS

1. Argument from the Poverty of the Stimulus:

  • Explanation: This argument, often associated with Noam Chomsky, posits that the linguistic input available to children is not rich enough to account for the complexity and precision of language acquisition. The argument suggests that there must be an innate linguistic capacity, as children seem to acquire language structures beyond what is directly presented to them.

2. Association Procedure:

  • Explanation: An approach to language learning that involves forming associations between stimuli and responses. In the context of language acquisition, this could mean associating words with their meanings through repeated exposure.

3. Connectionism:

  • Explanation: A theoretical framework in cognitive science and artificial intelligence that models mental or behavioral phenomena as the emergent properties of interconnected networks. In language acquisition, connectionist models simulate learning through interconnected nodes, mimicking the structure of neural networks.

4. Constraints on Form:

  • Explanation: Restrictions or rules governing the permissible structures and combinations of linguistic elements in a language. These constraints ensure that the form (structure) of language adheres to specific rules.

5. Constraints on Meaning:

  • Explanation: Restrictions or rules that govern the interpretation and meaning of linguistic expressions. These constraints ensure that the meaning conveyed by language follows specific rules or principles.

6. Critical Period:

  • Explanation: A specific timeframe during which an individual is most receptive to acquiring certain skills or knowledge. In language acquisition, there is evidence suggesting that there is a critical period for acquiring language, and beyond this period, language acquisition becomes more challenging.

7. Grammar as a Mental Generative Procedure:

  • Explanation: The concept that grammar is not just a set of rules but a mental mechanism that generates an infinite number of grammatically correct sentences. Chomsky's transformational-generative grammar is an example of this idea.

8. Imitation:

  • Explanation: The process of learning by copying or replicating the behavior or expressions of others. In language acquisition, imitation involves children learning language by replicating the speech patterns and expressions of those around them.

9. Negative Evidence:

  • Explanation: Information or feedback indicating that a linguistic expression is incorrect. In language acquisition, negative evidence helps learners understand what is grammatically incorrect or unacceptable.

10. Parameters:

  • Explanation: In the context of the Principles-and-Parameters model, parameters are innate settings that can be adjusted during language acquisition. They allow for linguistic variation across different languages.

11. Positive Evidence:

  • Explanation: Information or feedback indicating that a linguistic expression is correct. Positive evidence helps learners reinforce correct language forms and structures.

12. Principles:

  • Explanation: In the Principles-and-Parameters model, principles are innate linguistic rules or constraints that are common across all languages. They represent universal aspects of language structure.

13. Universal Grammar:

  • Explanation: The theory that the ability to acquire language is innate to humans, and there is a common underlying structure (Universal Grammar) that guides language acquisition. It includes principles and parameters.

  • Observation: Babies born into a multilingual environment can easily pick up more than one language.
  • Implication: Infants can distinguish between utterances in the language of their environment and those in other languages. They can also differentiate between utterances in multiple languages spoken around them.

4. Importance of Language Discrimination:

  • Concept: Learning a language involves discovering its rules, such as word order in clauses and the formation of questions.
  • Rationale: If infants couldn't distinguish between utterances in different languages, they might make incorrect assumptions about the linguistic properties they hear.

But how can researchers establish that young babies discriminate languages? The standard technique is a habituation-recovery procedure that exploits infants' sucking behavior: the high-amplitude sucking procedure (HAS) (see also Jusczyk 1997 for a description of this technique).

In a typical experiment using the HAS, infants suck on a pacifier, which is linked to a pressure transducer connected to a computer, enabling their sucking rate to be measured. Stimuli are presented through a loudspeaker. The more vigorously infants suck, the more stimuli they receive.

A typical setting is presented in Figure 2.1. An experiment using the HAS procedure starts by measuring infants' sucking rate in the absence of any stimulation, to determine each infant's baseline. Then the habituation phase starts. During this phase linguistic stimuli are presented, in general contingent on infants' sucking rate. This aspect of the experiment exploits the fact that infants are very good at learning the connection between sucking and sounds and then using sucking to trigger stimulation. When infants have heard the same stimulus for a while, they become habituated to it and suck less.

When the infant's sucking rate has reached a predetermined habituation criterion (a one-third decrement in sucking rate), the experimental phase starts. Infants are assigned to one of two conditions. In the experimental condition, infants are presented with new stimuli, while in the control condition, infants continue to receive the same stimuli they have heard in the habituation phase.

To demonstrate that infants discriminate between two sets of stimuli, one must compare the sucking responses of the experimental group with the sucking responses of the control group before and after the change. If infants in the experimental group are sufficiently interested by the new stimuli, they should suck more than infants in the control group. Since the only difference between the two groups before and after the change is the occurrence or nonoccurrence of new stimuli, a different response (increased sucking or lack thereof) by the two groups is interpreted as discrimination between the two sets of stimuli.

This technique can be used with infants up to 3 months of age.1 For concreteness, let us consider the experiment carried out by Mehler et al. (1988). In the habituation phase, infant French learners from both the experimental and the control groups heard Russian utterances. When they were habituated—that is, when their sucking rate declined to a predetermined criterion—the experimental phase started. During this phase, the control group continued to hear Russian utterances, and the experimental group started to hear French utterances. While the habituation phase was identical for the two groups of babies, the experimental phase differed. Therefore, a difference in the sucking responses of the experimental and control groups must be attributed to the single change that occurred during the experimental phase, that is, the delivery of new stimuli to the experimental group.

2.1.2 Discrimination between Two Foreign Languages

From the previous discussion, it is apparent that babies can discriminate their native language from a foreign language soon after birth. It is possible that language-specific influences have already left a trace after a few days of exposure or even that infants have already become familiar with certain properties of their linguistic environment before birth.2 However, neither possibility explains newborns' ability to distinguish between foreign languages, that is, languages they have not been exposed to.

Four-day-old infants born into a French-speaking environment distinguish between English and Italian utterances (see Mehler et al. 1988, reanalyzed in Mehler and Christophe 1995) and between English and Japanese utterances (see Nazzi, Bertoncini, and Mehler 1998; and see Cristophe and Morton 1998 for evidence that this also occurs at 2 months). A word of caution is needed, however. One might object to a developmental explanation since the pairs of languages used and the native languages of the babies tested vary across studies. Although we know that at 2 months infant English learners cannot discriminate French from Russian and French from Japanese, we do not know what they would have done at 4 days with the same pairs of languages. Moreover, we know that at 4 and 5 days infants born into a French-speaking environment can discriminate English from Italian and English from Japanese, respectively, but we do not know what they would do at 2 months. To be certain that the change in performance between younger and older babies is due to developmental factors, additional experimentation is needed.

2.1.3 The Source of Discrimination

We have seen that very young infants can discriminate between pairs of languages, even though they are not familiar with them; they do so on the basis of very little information. Dehaene-Lambertz and Houston (1998) showed that infants can recognize their native language after hearing 1200 ms of an utterance, or roughly half a dozen syllables (see also Bosch and Sebastia n-Galle s (1997)).

But how did the infants in these studies manage to discriminate between pairs of languages? It is very implausible that they could have recognized some words of the languages, because at 4 days, the age at which infants were tested, they are unlikely to have any lexical knowledge.

One possibility is that the babies responded to some coarse acoustic characteristics of the samples, such as the mean energy of the utterances or the pitch. To test this hypothesis, Mehler et al. (1988) presented the same French and Russian stimuli used in the experiment discussed in section 2.1.1, but played backward. In this way, the prosody of the utterance is modified, but the energy of the signal is the same. If babies exploit some gross acoustic property of speech, they should discriminate between languages even when the stimuli are played backward. This prediction did not stand up: under these conditions 4-day-old babies born into a French-speaking environment were unable to discriminate French from Russian.

Another possibility is that infants accomplish the discrimination task by recognizing segmental properties, like place of articulation or manner of articulation, of the languages. Indeed, in some of the experiments mentioned in sections 2.1.1 and 2.1.2 real-language samples were used.

To figure out whether infants draw upon segmental information to accomplish the language discrimination task, Mehler et al. (1988) and other researchers have tested infants with low-pass-filtered speech, that is, speech in which only frequencies below 400 Hz were retained. This transformation reduces segmental content (single sounds can no longer be identified) and preserves prosodic or suprasegmental information (intonation, rhythm).3 If infants were relying on segmental information, they should not have been able to discriminate between low-pass-filtered samples from different languages because, in these samples, the segmental information was gone. The finding that infants nonetheless succeeded under these conditions indicates that infants do not rely on segmental information to discriminate between languages but on some prosodic properties of speech.

Prosodic information is thus sufficient for telling languages apart. Is it also necessary? In low-pass-filtered speech, some rudimentary segmental information is indeed retained, and this may be enough for infants to discriminate between languages.

To discard this explanation, Dehaene-Lambertz and Houston (1998) presented infants with multisyllabic words extracted from sentences and reassembled in a scrambled order. The aim of this manipulation was to destroy the suprasegmental coherence of the utterance and to preserve phonemic information and information about the prosody of single words. If phonemic information or word-level prosody is enough to discriminate between two languages, babies listening to words reassembled in a scrambled order should have no trouble discriminating between languages. By contrast, if phonemic information or word-level prosody is not sufficient, babies listening to scrambled words should not be able to discriminate between languages. Indeed, 2-month-old American English learners failed to discriminate between English and French in the scrambled-order condition, but they succeeded when the prosodic information of the utterances was preserved as with low-pass-filtered stimuli. Thus, the prosodic information contained in brief (1200 ms) utterances is both sufficient and necessary for telling languages apart.

(Note that what matters is prosodic information at the level of the utterance and not at the level of single words. In fact, in the scrambled-order condition, the prosodic structure of words was available but did not elicit a discrimination response.)

Table 2.3 summarizes hypotheses, predictions, and results regarding language discrimination by babies.

Conclusion: Babies must discriminate languages on the basis of prosodic information at the utterance level.

Prosody in linguistics refers to the rhythm, pitch, and intonation patterns of speech. It encompasses the variations in loudness, pitch, tempo, and rhythm that shape the melody or musicality of spoken language.

2.1.4 The Rhythm-Based Language Discrimination Hypothesis

We have seen that infants rely on some prosodic information to discriminate between languages. Can we be more precise about the nature of the information they rely upon? Discrimination requires infants to build a representation of the first set of stimuli (utterances from one language), then compare it with a second set of stimuli (utterances from a new language), and determine whether these new stimuli match the earlier representation. This leads to a new question: what is the nature of the representation that infants rely on during a language discrimination task?

Mehler et al. (1996) propose that infants extract and build a representation of languages based on rhythmic properties, which are known to vary across languages. Dutch and English sound very different from Spanish and Italian, a difference that Lloyd James (1940) described by saying that the first two languages have a "Morse code rhythm'' and the second two a "machine gun rhythm.'' Building on this intuition, linguists have coarsely classified languages into stress-timed, syllable-timed, and mora-timed languages.

(1)

  1. Stress-timed languages: Dutch, English, Russian, Swedish
  2. Syllable-timed languages: Italian, French, Greek, Spanish
  3. Mora-timed languages: Japanese, Tamil

What distinguishes these classes of languages is the subjective perception of their temporal organization. In stress-timed languages listeners perceive a regular recurrence of stress, in syllable-timed languages a regular recurrence of syllables, and in mora-timed languages a regular recurrence of morae. It is now believed that this distinction in classes of languages is not a primitive property, but the result of interactions between phonological properties of languagesÐfor example, syllable structure and vowel reduction. Stress-timed languages have a greater variety of syllable types than syllable-timed languages; in these languages heavy syllables tend to be stressed and light ones to be stressless.

On the basis of investigating eight languages (Catalan, Dutch, English, French, Italian, Japanese, Polish, Spanish), Ramus, Nespor, and Mehler (1999) and Ramus and Mehler (1999) have shown that the distinction among the three rhythmic classes in (1) re¯ects phonetic properties of the speech signal, thus supporting the validity of the notion of rhythmic class.

As noted, Mehler et al. (1996) propose that infants discriminate between languages on the basis of a rhythmic representation (the rhythm-based language discrimination hypothesis) (see also Nazzi, Bertoncini, and Mehler (1998)). The rhythmic representation is a coarse representation in terms of "a succession of vowels of variable duration and intensities, alternating with periods of unanalyzed noise'' (standing for consonants) (Ramus, Nespor, and Mehler 1999, 270). This hypothesis grants a central role to vowels. In fact, vowels are acoustically very salient: they carry most of the energy of the speech signal, are louder than consonants, and attract infants' attention more than consonants do.

Infants' representation of utterances as a sequence of vowels varies depending on the rhythmic properties of the language, that is, whether it is stress-timed, syllable-timed, or mora-timed. (2) is an approximate depiction of infants' rhythmic representations, where V stands for a heavy vowel and v for a light one.

(2)

  1. V Vv VVvV stress-timed (e.g., English)
  2. V VV V VV V syllable-timed (e.g., Spanish)
  3. VVVVVVVVV mora-timed (e.g., Japanese)

In stress-timed languages, (2a), the interval between vowels is long and irregular, because of these languages' greater variability of syllable structure. In stress-timed languages there is great variability in the number of consonants per syllable; for example, English and Dutch have 16 and 19 syllable types and a maximum of seven segments per syllable (see Nespor 1990). Finally, in these languages there is great variability in the duration of syllables.

In syllable-timed languages, (2b), the distance between vowels is shorter and more regular since there are few types of syllables. For example, Spanish has 9 syllable types that contain at most five segments (see Nespor 1990); Italian and Greek have 8 syllable types.

In mora-timed languages, (2c), the distance between vowels is even shorter than in syllable-timed languages and even more regular, for these languages have long vowels, which correspond to two regular vowels.

2.1.4.1 Evidence for the Rhythm-Based Language Discrimination Hypothesis

According to the hypothesis that infants build a representation of languages in terms of rhythm and that they use rhythmic information to discriminate between pairs of languages, infants classify languages on the basis of their global rhythmic properties and disregard the specific identity of a language; that is, they classify languages within rhythmic classes. This hypothesis predicts that infants can discriminate between languages with different metrical patterns, but not between languages with the same metrical patternÐfor example, two stress-timed languages such as Dutch and English.

The predictions (P) are summarized in (3).

(3)

  • P1: Infants discriminate stress-timed from syllable-timed languages.
  • P2: Infants discriminate stress-timed from mora-timed languages.
  • P3: Infants discriminate syllable-timed from mora-timed languages.
  • P4: Infants do not discriminate (at least initially) two syllable-/stress-/mora-timed languages from each other.

As shown in section 2.1.1, newborns can discriminate their native language from a wide variety of languages. Looking back at table 2.1 through the lens of the rhythm-based language discrimination hypothesis, we realize that three of the predictions in (3) are fulfilled.

(4) P1: Infants discriminate stress-timed from syllable-timed languages: English-Catalan, English-Italian, English-French, English-Spanish, French-Russian. P2: Infants discriminate stress-timed from mora-timed languages: English-Japanese. P4: Infants do not discriminate (at least initially) two syllable-/stress-/mora-timed languages from each other: English-Dutch (stress-timed languages).

So far no experiment has tested infants' ability to discriminate a syllable-timed from a mora-timed language with babies exposed to either a syllable-timed or a mora-timed language. Therefore, we have no evidence bearing on prediction P3.

The findings summarized in (4) provide ample evidence that infants discriminate between languages from different rhythmic classes. Interestingly, newborns living in a French-speaking environment cannot discriminate between two foreign languages from the same rhythmic class: Dutch and English (see Nazzi, Bertoncini, and Mehler 1998; see table 2.2). The same finding is replicated with 2-month-old British English learners (see Christophe and Morton 1998; see table 2.1). These results support the hypothesis that the rhythmic information infants rely upon is class-specific and not language-specific. Eventually, at 5 months, babies learning English are able to discriminate Dutch from English, likely because they have become more familiar with their native tongue or have access to a more detailed representation of their language (see Nazzi, Jusczyk, and Johnson 2000; see table 2.1). The same explanation can be extended to account for why 4-month-old Catalan and Spanish learners can discriminate between Spanish and Catalan, two syllable-timed languages. It would be interesting to know how these babies would have responded at 4 days.

The most convincing confirmation of the rhythm-based language discrimination hypothesis comes from an experiment carried out by Nazzi, Bertoncini, and Mehler (1998). In this experiment filtered utterances were drawn from four languages: two syllable-timed languages (Italian and Spanish) and two stress-timed languages (Dutch and English). No language was familiar to the babies tested, who were from a French-speaking environment. Four-day-old infants were assigned to one of two groups. The rhythmic group was habituated to a combination of filtered sentences from two languages of the same rhythmic class (Italian/Spanish or Dutch/English); the nonrhythmic group was habituated to a combination of filtered sentences from two languages belonging to different rhythmic classes (English/Italian or Dutch/Spanish). During the experimental phase, the infants in the rhythmic group experienced a class change: they listened to filtered sentences from two languages belonging to the other rhythmic class (if they first heard Italian/Spanish, they then heard Dutch/English, and vice versa). The infants in the nonrhythmic group also listened to new stimuli, but these were drawn from the same two rhythmic classes used in the habituation phase (if these infants first heard English/Italian, they then heard Dutch/Spanish, and vice versa). The design is summarized in table 2.4. The main finding of the experiment is that after the change the rhythmic group started to suck significantly more than the nonrhythmic group. Therefore, it must be inferred that only the rhythmic group detected a change in stimuli, that is, a change from one rhythmic class to the other. As the stimuli presented to the nonrhythmic group also changedÐthe individual languages were not the same in the habituation and experimental phases it must be deduced that this change did not result in a discrimination response. The conclusion must be that infants discriminate between classes of languages and not individual languages.

2.1.5 The Syllabic or Vocalic Nucleus as a Universal Unit of Representation of Speech

The rhythm-based language discrimination hypothesis proposes that infants perceive and represent speech using syllable-like units, particularly the vowel. This suggests that the syllable or the vocalic nucleus serves as a universal unit for infants in organizing speech.

Evidence Supporting the Hypothesis

Three studies provide support for this hypothesis. First, 4-day-olds in a French-speaking environment can distinguish between changes from bisyllabic to trisyllabic items but not changes in the number of phonemes with the same syllable count. Second, these infants can discriminate between bi- and trisyllabic Japanese items but not between bisyllabic items with varying morae. In a different experiment, newborns were exposed to syllables sharing the same vowel or consonant, and they detected changes when the vowel changed but not when the consonant changed.

Universal Role of Vowels in Representation

These results support the idea that infants build a representation based on vowels, which are highly salient nuclei of syllables carrying acoustic information about length, intensity, and height. This indicates that the syllable or vocalic nucleus is a likely universal unit in the early stages of language development.

Previous Studies and Syllabic Discrimination

Earlier research with premature babies also emphasized the role of the syllable in discrimination. Studies showed that 4-day-olds in a French-speaking environment could discern changes from bisyllabic to trisyllabic items and discriminate between bi- and trisyllabic Japanese items, further supporting the role of the syllable or vowel in early speech perception.

2.1.6 Intermediate Summary

Infants exhibit a remarkably specialized ability to process speech input. By 4 days old, they can discriminate their native language from a foreign one and even distinguish between two foreign languages. This discrimination ability is grounded in the rhythmic properties of speech.

In the early stages, newborns construct a basic and underspecified rhythmic representation of speech, focusing on syllables or vowels—distinct and salient units that capture their attention. This initial representation is sufficiently robust for an initial classification of languages into rhythmic classes.

This rudimentary rhythmic representation lays the foundation for developing a more nuanced and detailed representation that aligns closely with the phonological properties of their native language. The process involves a progression from a broad rhythmic classification to a more refined understanding of the intricacies of the native language's phonology.

2.2 LEARNING THE PHONEMIC CONTRASTS OF THE NATIVE LANGUAGE

Infants differentiate languages not just by rhythm but also by the sounds used in words. Languages vary in the 20 to 60 phonemes they employ. For instance, English has about 40 phonemes, while Italian has 33. Some languages, like Japanese, merge sounds like /l/ and /r/.

Phonetic Differences

  • English: ~40 phonemes
  • Italian: 33 phonemes
  • Japanese: Merges /l/ and /r/ into /R/

Adults excel at native language phonemic distinctions but may struggle with non-native ones. How infants navigate this sound diversity sheds light on their language development.

Infant Perception of Consonants

Two theories emerge on how infants acquire language sounds:

  1. Blank slate - infants learn language-specific discriminations.
  2. Inherent capacity - infants can discriminate all sounds, learning to ignore non-relevant ones.

Next, we delve into infants' perception of consonants to unravel this mystery.

2.2.1 Sensitivity to Consonant Contrasts

Early Consonant Discrimination

  • Infants exhibit sensitivity to consonant contrasts very early in life.
  • Eimas et al. (1971) demonstrated that, from around 1 month of age, English-speaking infants can discriminate between voiced [ba] and voiceless [pa] sounds, akin to bad and pad.

Categorical Perception

  • Infants' perception of consonantal contrasts is categorical, akin to adults.
  • They distinguish between different linguistic categories but struggle with differences within the same category.

Range of Consonant Discrimination

  • Further research has confirmed infants' ability to discriminate in terms of place of articulation (e.g., [ba] vs. [ga]) and manner of articulation (oral/nasal) (e.g., [ba] vs. [ma]).

Native vs. Non-native Contrasts

  • Challenge to the notion that experience shapes discrimination arises from Werker and Tees (1984).
  • 6- to 8-month-old infants can discriminate non-native consonantal contrasts, challenging the idea that experience solely drives this ability.

Beyond Native Language Exposure

  • Infants can discern contrasts in foreign languages, such as Hindi and Thompson, even without exposure.
  • Adult loss of this skill suggests infants converge on adult competence with native language phonemic distinctions, but the timeline remains an open question.

2.2.2 Developmental Changes in the Perception of Nonnative Contrasts

Decline in Discrimination Ability

  • Newborns' remarkable ability to discriminate unfamiliar phonemic contrasts declines rapidly.
  • At 6–8 months, infants can still discriminate non native contrasts, but by 8–10 months, success decreases. By 10–12 months, they are no longer able to discriminate, resembling English-speaking adults.

2.2.3 Forgetting as a Way of Developing a Phonological System

Developmental Change at 12 Months

  • Around 12 months, infants undergo a developmental change in their phonological system.
  • Werker and Pegg (1992) and Werker (1995) propose a functional reorganization of the sound space.

Reorganization Hypothesis

  • Loss of sensitivity to foreign contrasts is not due to auditory system changes but a postperceptual process.
  • The reorganization enables infants to attend only to sounds with phonemic value, aiding in distinguishing meanings and learning words.

Predisposition to Phonemic Contrasts

  • Infants become predisposed to attend only to contrasts that distinguish meanings in their language.
  • Contrasts lacking phonemic value are disregarded, streamlining the process of building a lexicon and learning words.

Role in Word Learning

  • Functional reorganization is part of the program facilitating word learning and the acquisition of linguistic objects.
  • Likely responsible for infants' sensitivity to various segmental information and incorporation of language-specific segments into linguistic productions around 8–10 months.

Coordination of Capacities

  • Suggestive evidence indicates a coordinated schedule guiding infants to acquire precisely the information needed for word acquisition and subsequent linguistic milestones.

Gain for the Learner

  • Although the decline in sensitivity might seem like a loss, it is viewed as a gain for the learner.
  • Restricts the search space, minimizing chances of errors and preparing learners for new achievements.

2.2.4 Early Second Language Acquisition

Functional Reorganization Hypothesis

  • Loss of sensitivity to foreign contrasts is not permanent, according to the functional reorganization hypothesis.
  • Early learners of a second language resemble native speakers, while adults learning a foreign language often retain a foreign accent, more pronounced with later learning.

Age-Related Effects

  • Foreign accent detected in children exposed to a second language before age 4.
  • Age affects both production and perception, with late learners less proficient in discriminating phonemic contrasts of the second language.

Reactivation of System

  • System for detecting foreign contrasts seems to be reactivated in young learners, at least up to a certain age.
  • The later a language is learned, the more discernible a foreign accent becomes.

Exceptions to Reactivation

  • Recent research suggests reactivation may not occur for certain contrasts.
  • Spanish-Catalan bilingual adults (Spanish-dominant) do not discriminate a specific Catalan vowel contrast, assimilating Catalan phonemes to Spanish ones.
  • Permanent loss of sensitivity to some foreign contrasts challenges the functional reorganization hypothesis.

Unanswered Questions

  • Research needs to establish whether the permanent loss concerns specific sounds and under what conditions.
  • Crucial for refining or abandoning the functional reorganization hypothesis.

2.2.5 The Nature of Infants' Perception Ability

Specialized Ability in Language Acquisition

  • Infants possess a specialized ability for discriminating all possible phonetic contrasts in a categorical manner.
  • With experience, infants become attuned to contrasts with phonemic value in their native language.

Categorical Perception Debate

  • Debate exists on whether categorical perception is language-specific or reflects a general acoustic process.
  • Some argue that languages take advantage of auditory perception system by placing phoneme boundaries at auditory sensitivity peaks.

Acoustic Sensitivity

  • Not all speech sounds are equally salient; some are more prominent and easily categorized by the auditory system.
  • The human auditory system is most sensitive between 2000 and 5000 Hz, while speech sounds are produced between 100 and 6400 Hz.
  • Phonemic distinctions are generally placed in highly sensitive regions, indicating exploitation of human perceptual capacities.

Categorical Perception in Animals

  • Animals like chinchillas and macaques can perceive speech sounds categorically, suggesting a general acoustic ability.
  • Preliminary evidence suggests cotton-top tamarin monkeys can discriminate pairs of languages, similar to human infants.

Language-Specific Exploitation

  • While certain abilities may not be language-specific, it is in humans that these skills are exploited to acquire language and map linguistic structures.

2.2.6 Intermediate Summary

Infants' Speech Perception Ability

  • At birth, infants exhibit the ability to discriminate a wide variety of sound contrasts.
  • This ability is crucial, enabling infants to cope with the phonological properties of any language and learn languages they are exposed to.

Developmental Changes

  • Infants' speech perception ability undergoes changes through exposure to the linguistic environment.
  • By 12 months, infants resemble adults in no longer being able to perform certain discriminations, a change essential for acquiring words.
